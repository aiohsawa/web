- P1: Initial Pressure
- T1: Initial Temperature
- P2: Final Pressure
- T2: Final Temperature

- Named after Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac
- Announced in 1808
- The relationship between pressure and temperature is directly proportional if the volume is kept constant.
- An example of Gay-Lussac’s Law is when you heat a pressure cooker, the temperature inside rises. As the temperature increases, the gas molecules inside the cooker move faster and collide with the walls more forcefully, increasing the pressure.
- Another example of Gay-Lussac’s Law is when you leave a sealed can of spray paint in the sun, the temperature of the gas inside the can rises. According to Gay-Lussac's Law, as the temperature increases, the pressure inside the can also increases. If the pressure becomes too high, the can could potentially burst. This is why it’s important to store pressurized cans in a cool, shaded place.

Example problem: A sealed container with air has an initial pressure of 2.0 atm while the temperature is 20°C. When the temperature drops to 4°C, what is the final pressure inside the jar?
Given:
P1: 2.0 atm
T1: 20.0°C / 293K
P2: ?
T2: 4°C / 277K

Solution:
20 + 273 = 293
4 + 273 = 277
P1/T1 = P2/T2
2/293 = ? / 277
(2)(277)/293
554/293 = 1.89atm
P2= 1.89atm
